\section{Data Exploration \& Preprocessing}
\label{sec:eda}

The dataset contains 35\,000 training and 15\,000 test applicants with 55 features spanning demographics, employment, assets/liabilities, credit history, and loan request. Two targets are predicted jointly: \textbf{RiskTier} (5-way classification, \(\{0,1,2,3,4\}\)) and \textbf{InterestRate} (regression in \([4.99,\,35.99]\%\)). The Kaggle leaderboard scores \(0.5\cdot\mathrm{Accuracy} + 0.5\cdot R^2\); the baseline linear models achieve \(\sim\!0.51\).

\paragraph{Key findings.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RiskTier is balanced.} Counts are 6\,724 / 7\,283 / 6\,998 / 6\,812 / 7\,183 for tiers 0--4 (Figure~\ref{fig:targets}, left). No class weighting or SMOTE was needed.
    \item \textbf{InterestRate is multi-modal with a floor.} Mean 7.31\%, median 6.08\%, but 33\% of rows are exactly at the legal floor of 4.99\% (Figure~\ref{fig:targets}, middle). Tiers 0--3 have rate means between 6.01 and 6.56\% with \(\sigma \approx 1.5\); tier 4 has mean 11.55\% and \(\sigma = 7.36\), producing a long right tail (Figure~\ref{fig:targets}, right). This two-regime structure (``prime'' vs.\ ``subprime'') shaped much of our modelling.
    \item \textbf{Variance decomposition.} Within-tier variance in rate explains 72\% of total rate variance; between-tier only 28\%. Perfect RiskTier prediction alone therefore caps \(R^2\) at \(\approx 0.28\) --- accurate regression must capture within-tier rate drivers, not just tier identity.
    \item \textbf{Structural missingness.} The PDF explicitly flagged this and the data confirm it (Figure~\ref{fig:missing}): \texttt{StudentLoanOutstandingBalance} is missing 60\% of the time (older applicants), \texttt{CollateralType} 55\% (unsecured loans), \texttt{PropertyValue} and \texttt{MortgageOutstandingBalance} 45\% (renters). Verified empirically: 99\% of rows with \texttt{HomeOwnership = RENT} have missing \texttt{PropertyValue}.
    \item \textbf{Money variables are right-skewed log-normal.} \texttt{AnnualIncome}, balances, and asset values benefit from a \(\log(1+x)\) transform before tree training.
\end{itemize}

\section{Feature Engineering}
\label{sec:feateng}

We grew the original 55 features into roughly 170 model inputs across six groups. Every engineered feature is documented with financial intuition in \texttt{code/features.py} and grouped so the ablation study (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) can isolate contribution.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Balance-sheet.} \(\texttt{feat\_NetWorth}= \text{assets}-\text{liabilities}\); \(\texttt{feat\_DebtToAssets}\); \(\texttt{feat\_LiquidToLoan}= (\text{Savings}+\text{Checking}) / \text{RequestedLoan}\); \(\texttt{feat\_CashRunwayMonths}\); \(\texttt{feat\_MortgageLTV}\). Financial intuition: underwriters assess leverage and liquidity buffer, not just income.
    \item \textbf{Credit behaviour.} \(\texttt{feat\_DelinquencyScore} = 1\!\cdot\!\text{Late30} + 3\!\cdot\!\text{Late60} + 9\!\cdot\!\text{Late90}\) mirrors FICO severity weighting. \(\texttt{feat\_DerogatoryScore}\) uses \(10\!\cdot\!\text{Bankruptcies} + 5\!\cdot\!\text{ChargeOffs} + 4\!\cdot\!\text{PublicRecords} + 3\!\cdot\!\text{Collections}\). Composite \(\texttt{feat\_BadEventsTotal}\) is the single strongest RiskTier predictor in SHAP analysis.
    \item \textbf{Credit maturity.} \(\texttt{feat\_ThinFile}\) (history $<$ 24mo), \(\texttt{feat\_ThickFile}\) ($>$120mo), \(\texttt{feat\_NewAccountSpree} = \text{Oldest}-\text{AvgAge}\), \(\texttt{feat\_CreditAgeRatio}\). Thin-file borrowers are systematically riskier at matched DTI.
    \item \textbf{Demographics + stability.} \(\texttt{feat\_EmpStability} = \text{EmpLength}/(\text{Age}-18)\); \(\texttt{feat\_SameEmployerShare}\); \(\texttt{feat\_StableResident}\) ($>$3 yrs); \(\texttt{feat\_HasSecondaryIncome}\); \(\texttt{feat\_IncomeVerified}\).
    \item \textbf{Loan request.} \(\texttt{feat\_MonthlyPrincipal}\); \(\texttt{feat\_LoanToAssets}\); \(\texttt{feat\_PaymentReserveMonths}\); \(\texttt{feat\_HasCollateral}\); \(\texttt{feat\_RepeatCustomer}\).
    \item \textbf{Interactions.} \(\texttt{feat\_DTI\_x\_Util}\), \(\texttt{feat\_LTI\_x\_Term}\), \(\texttt{feat\_PTI\_x\_BadEvents}\), \(\texttt{feat\_DerogPerHistoryYear}\). Encode known nonlinear compounds (high DTI $\times$ high utilisation, long term $\times$ high LTI).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Multi-target encoding.} For the three high-cardinality categoricals (\texttt{State}, \texttt{JobCategory}, \texttt{LoanPurpose}), we encode each group with three statistics: mean \(\text{InterestRate}\), \(P(\text{tier}=4)\), and per-group rate \(\sigma\). This triples the signal vs.\ single-mean encoding. K-fold computation on train / full-train on test protects against leakage; an interesting artefact is discussed in \S\ref{sec:models} (adversarial AUC = 1.00).

\section{Model Selection \& Tuning}
\label{sec:models}

\paragraph{Models compared.} We evaluated every candidate on the same 5-fold \texttt{StratifiedKFold} on RiskTier, with identical fold indices reused for Task B so out-of-fold predictions align across tasks (Table~\ref{tab:models}). This alignment is the foundation of the cross-task stacking that powers our final ensemble.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{p{5.0cm}ccp{6.0cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{Task A Acc} & \textbf{Task B $R^2$} & \textbf{Notes} \\
\midrule
Logistic / Linear Regression            & 0.538 & 0.502 & PDF baseline $\approx 0.51$ combined. \\
LightGBM                                & 0.794 & 0.833 & LR 0.02, num\_leaves 127, L1+L2 reg. \\
XGBoost                                 & 0.792 & 0.831 & LR 0.02, max\_depth 8, hist method. \\
CatBoost                                & \textbf{0.809} & \textbf{0.834} & Best single model; native cats +0.002. \\
LGB regression-on-tier (ordinal trick)  & 0.791 & ---   & Round \& clip to class at inference. \\
Two-stage Task A (binary + 4-class)     & 0.796 & ---   & Binary $\text{is\_tier}4\,\rightarrow\,$4-class on prime. \\
Stage-2 meta-LGBM (single seed)         & 0.837 & 0.840 & Input: 170 base features + 40 OOF features. \\
\textbf{Stage-2 multi-seed + Ridge blend} & \textbf{0.840} & \textbf{0.841} & \textbf{Our champion: combined 0.8407.} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\label{tab:models}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Cross-task stacking.} The dominant architectural choice. Since \(\text{RiskTier}\) and \(\text{InterestRate}\) are nearly collinear, OOF predictions of each task carry information the other's feature matrix lacks. We produce OOF class probabilities for Task A (5 floats per row) and scalar OOF rate predictions for Task B, concatenate them with the 170 original features, and retrain a second-stage LightGBM per task. The stage-2 model consistently dominates the final convex blend (0.80--0.95 weight), confirming it internalises all the ensemble diversity the stage-1 models provide.

\paragraph{Overfitting controls.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Early stopping with patience 200 on a held-out fold for every model.
    \item K-fold target encoding (fold \(i\) encoded from folds \(\neq i\)) prevents leakage from InterestRate into training features.
    \item \texttt{feature\_fraction} = 0.75, \texttt{bagging\_fraction} = 0.80 on LightGBM for tree diversity.
    \item Winsorisation caps predictor extremes; multi-seed (42, 1337, 2024) averaging on the stage-2 meta-learner reduces variance.
    \item \textbf{Adversarial validation.} We trained a binary classifier to distinguish train from test rows and observed OOF AUC = 1.00 --- the two sets were perfectly separable. The culprit was our multi-target encoder: train values are noisier (K-fold) than test values (full-train mean). An \texttt{iter7} experiment dropped the 20 worst offenders; the result was essentially flat (+0.0001), indicating the stage-2 model already implicitly down-weighted them. We report this artefact because it exemplifies the value of adversarial validation even when the fix is small.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Do the same features matter for both tasks?} Largely yes, but with clear differences. Both tasks' top-10 importance lists share \(\texttt{feat\_BadEventsTotal}\), \(\texttt{DebtToIncomeRatio}\), \(\texttt{PaymentToIncomeRatio}\), \(\texttt{RevolvingUtilizationRate}\), and \(\texttt{feat\_DelinquencyScore}\). Task A further prefers structural binary flags (\(\texttt{feat\_IsHomeOwner}\), \(\texttt{feat\_HasCollateral}\), \(\texttt{IncomeVerified}\)) that sharply separate tiers; Task B weights continuous leverage ratios (\(\texttt{feat\_LoanToAssets}\), \(\texttt{feat\_MortgageLTV}\), \(\texttt{feat\_CashRunwayMonths}\)) more highly, consistent with its task of pricing within a tier.

\paragraph{Iteration ladder (Figure~\ref{fig:ladder}).} We ran 14 iterations combining a variety of published Kaggle techniques. Most advanced tricks yielded essentially zero improvement over the basic stage-2 ensemble: \emph{tier-4 mixture-of-experts} regressed because each expert sees less data; \emph{monotone constraints} with the ``advanced'' enforcement mode were too restrictive and cost 0.03 accuracy on LightGBM; a \emph{rate-floor two-stage} model failed because the floor classifier's features are indistinguishable from just-above-floor rows (AUC 0.65); \emph{DART} misbehaved without early stopping; a \emph{tabular MLP} earned only 0.016 ensemble weight. The techniques that did help --- pseudo-labeling of 5\,000 confident test rows ($+0.0015$), adversarial-validation feature pruning ($+0.0001$), and KNN target features ($+0.0002$) --- collectively added only 0.0018 combined on top of the base stage-2 ensemble. The final submitted score of 0.8407 is clear evidence of a \textbf{feature ceiling}: every gradient-boosting variant we tried converged to the same narrow OOF band (0.838--0.841). Closing the gap to top teams ($\approx$0.88) would require fundamentally new features or features we cannot access under the ``no external credit data'' rule.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/fig_ladder.pdf}
    \caption{\textbf{Iteration ladder.} Green bars improved over the current champion, red regressed. After \texttt{iter8} (pseudo-labeling + KNN target features), no subsequent technique moved the needle. Dashed line: top-ranked team \emph{The Lions} at 0.88175.}
    \label{fig:ladder}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Conclusions.} A carefully tuned three-way boosting ensemble (LightGBM + XGBoost + CatBoost) with shared folds and cross-task stacking reaches a combined score of \(\approx\!0.84\) on 5-fold OOF --- well above the PDF's Random-Forest benchmark (0.76) and within 5\% of the top leaderboard entry. Our main learning is negative: the techniques that sound most sophisticated (mixture-of-experts, monotone constraints, tabular neural networks, second-round pseudo-labeling) did not improve this dataset. The highest-impact interventions were the least glamorous: a large set of financially motivated engineered features, clean K-fold target encoding, and a disciplined stage-2 stacker.
